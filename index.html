Procedure (Project Overview)

 

  • The study was a randomized double-blind controlled placebo (sham tDCS) crossover trial. Patients with obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and matched healthy control participants were invited for two sessions (crossover design), differing only with respect to the stimulation condition (sham vs. cathodal stimulation; order of stimulation conditions was counterbalanced).

 

  • Following administration of self-report questionnaires / clinical inventories, participants received cathodal tDCS or sham tDCS.
    • All participants completed the following questionnaires: OCI-R, BDI-II, Harm Avoidance scale of the TCI , STAI, PSWQ, FMPS, PANAS, Neuroticism and Conscientiousness scale of the NEO-FFI, BIS/BAS
    • Severity of OCD symptoms and of depressive symptoms were assessed in all patients using Y-BOCS and MADRS.

 

  • Following stimulation, participants performed an arrow version of the flanker interference task (see below) and a speeded go/no-go task with an embedded word categorization task (see GNG analysis folder).
    • During the tasks, behavioral performance, EEG, and activity of the autonomic nervous system (heart rate and skin conductance) were assessed.

 

Flanker Task

 

  • Participants performed an arrow version of the flanker interference task.
    • They were instructed to respond as quickly and accurately as possible to the direction of a centrally located horizontal target arrow, which was flanked by four horizontal distractor arrows. In half of the trials, the target was flanked by arrows pointing in the same direction (congruent trials), in the other half by arrows pointing in the opposite direction (incongruent trials).
    • During the task, behavioral performance and the electroencephalogram (EEG) were assessed.

 

Hypotheses

 

  • Patients with OCD exhibit an enhanced error-related negativity (ERN) compared to healthy control participants.  

  • Compared to sham stimulation, cathodal tDCS over the medial-frontal cortex attenuates the ERN amplitude, increases the error rate and the error positivity (Pe) amplitude, and reduces post-error slowing and the number of correct second responses (= replication Reinhart & Woodman, 2014).  

  • The stimulation effect, in particular the attenuation of the ERN amplitude, is more pronounced in patients with OCD compared to the control group (see Klawohn, Endrass, Preuss, Riesel, & Kathmann, 2016).  

  • Inhibitory effects of the cathodal stimulation can only be found for unmedicated patients (= pre-planned subgroup analysis, since SSRIs may change inhibitory effects of cathodal stimulation to an excitatory effect, see Nitsche et al., 2009).

 

Exploratory Hypotheses

 

  • We may aim to explore whether the ERN amplitude is positively related to the skin conductance response (SCR) and to post-error slowing on a single-trial level.
  • We may additionally investigate whether cathodal stimulation attenuates the SCR related to errors.
  • We may explore group differences in the SCR in response to errors and in the correct-related negativity (CRN) as well as the effect of tDCS on these measures and on group differences.

 

Design

 

  • 2 x 2 x 2 design: stimulation condition (verum, sham) and response type (correct, incorrect) as within-participant factors, group (HC, OCD) as between-participant factor.

 

Data Description

 

  • Data set: ‘Single_Trial_Data.rda’
  • 53760 observations (56 participants, 2 sessions per participant, 480 trials per session), 17 variables
